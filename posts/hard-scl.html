Supervised Contrastive Learning with Hard Negative Samples

Ruijie Jiang · May 10, 2024 · 10 min read
Contrastive Learning Hard Negatives Supervised Learning Representation Learning

This post walks through our paper "Supervised Contrastive Learning with Hard Negative Samples" (Jiang, Nguyen, Ishwar, Aeron, 2024). The central idea: what happens when you combine label information with hard-negative sampling in contrastive learning? The answer turns out to be both theoretically elegant and practically powerful.

1. Motivation

Contrastive learning has two main flavors: unsupervised (UCL), which treats all other samples as negatives, and supervised (SCL), which uses labels to ensure only different-class samples serve as negatives. Meanwhile, hard-negative mining — focusing on the most confusing negatives — has been shown to dramatically improve learning efficiency.

But here's the gap: prior work on hard negatives (like H-UCL from Robinson et al.) operates without labels, which means it can accidentally treat same-class samples as hard negatives — the infamous false negative problem. Standard SCL uses labels but treats all negatives equally, wasting effort on easy negatives that provide little learning signal.

The Key Insight

H-SCL is the first method to jointly leverage label information (to avoid false negatives) and hard-negative sampling (to focus on informative negatives). It gets the best of both worlds.

2. The Four Contrastive Variants

To understand H-SCL, it helps to see it in the context of four contrastive learning variants:

  • UCL — random negatives, no labels
  • SCL — label-filtered negatives (only different-class), no hardening
  • H-UCL — hardened negatives, no labels (suffers from false negatives)
  • H-SCL — hardened + label-filtered negatives (our method)

The relationships form a 2×2 grid: one axis is "use labels or not", the other is "use hardening or not". H-SCL occupies the optimal corner.

3. How H-SCL Works

3.1 The Hardening Function

A hardening function $\eta: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is any nonnegative, nondecreasing function. It reweights the negative sampling distribution to favor samples that are more similar to the anchor in representation space. Two concrete choices:

  • Exponential: $\eta_{\text{exp}}(t) = e^{\beta t}$ where $\beta > 0$ — smoothly upweights harder negatives
  • Threshold: $\eta_{\text{thresh}}(t) = \mathbf{1}(e^t \geq \tau)$ — binary filter keeping only the hardest negatives

3.2 The H-SCL Sampling Distribution

H-SCL draws negatives from a distribution that combines label filtering with hardening:

$$q_{\text{H-SCL}}(x^- | x, f) = \frac{\mathbf{1}(y(x^-) \neq y(x)) \cdot \eta(g(x, x^-)) \cdot q_{\text{UCL}}(x^-)}{\alpha_{\text{H-SCL}}(x, f)}$$

where $g(x, x^-) = f(x)^\top f(x^-) / \gamma$ is the normalized similarity. This distribution has three components:

  1. $\mathbf{1}(y(x^-) \neq y(x))$ — label filter: only consider samples from different classes
  2. $\eta(g(x, x^-))$ — hardening: upweight negatives that are closer in representation space
  3. $\alpha_{\text{H-SCL}}$ — normalization constant
Intuition

Think of it as a two-stage filter: first remove same-class samples (avoid false negatives), then focus on the remaining negatives that the model finds most confusing (maximize learning signal).

4. Main Theoretical Result

The paper's central theoretical contribution relates the losses of H-SCL and H-UCL. First, we need an assumption about the quality of representations:

Assumption 1 — Good Representation Quality

For hard-to-distinguish samples, collision samples (same class as anchor) have higher expected similarity than true negatives (different class):

$$\mathbb{E}[e^{g(x, x^-)} \mid x^- \sim q_{\text{Hcol}}] \geq \mathbb{E}[e^{g(x, x^-)} \mid x^- \sim q_{\text{H-SCL}}]$$

This says: among the hard samples, same-class ones are on average more similar to the anchor than different-class ones. This is naturally satisfied when representations have reasonable quality.

Lemma 1 — H-UCL Upper Bounds H-SCL

Under Assumption 1, in the infinite negative sample limit:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{H-UCL}}^{(\infty)}(f) \geq \mathcal{L}_{\text{H-SCL}}^{(\infty)}(f)$$

The H-UCL loss is always at least as large as the H-SCL loss.

Why does this matter? It tells us that minimizing H-UCL implicitly minimizes an upper bound on H-SCL. Since H-SCL is the "ideal" loss (using labels + hardening), this explains why H-UCL works well in practice even without labels — it's optimizing a bound on the quantity we really care about.

Proof Sketch

The key decomposition splits the H-UCL normalization constant into two parts:

$$\alpha_{\text{H-UCL}}(x, f) = \alpha_{\text{H-SCL}}(x, f) + \alpha_{\text{Hcol}}(x, f)$$

The H-UCL expectation is then a mixture of contributions from true negatives (H-SCL part) and collisions (same-class part). Assumption 1 ensures the collision contribution increases the loss, establishing the inequality.

5. Experimental Results

5.1 Image Classification

On standard benchmarks (STL-10, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100), H-SCL consistently outperforms SCL:

  • CIFAR-100: H-SCL achieves ~73% accuracy vs. SCL ~70% — a significant 3-point gain
  • Threshold hardening $\eta_{\text{thresh}}(\tau)$ and exponential hardening $\eta_{\text{exp}}(\beta)$ both show consistent improvements
  • Improvements hold across different hyperparameter settings

5.2 Verifying the Theory

The experiments validate both the assumption and the theorem:

  • Assumption 1 verification: The fraction of anchors satisfying the assumption increases during training (60-70% by end), confirming representations become "good" as training progresses
  • Loss inequality: $\mathcal{L}_{\text{H-SCL}} < \mathcal{L}_{\text{H-UCL}}$ holds consistently throughout training, validating Lemma 1

5.3 Beyond Vision: Graph Datasets

H-SCL benefits extend beyond image classification to graph-structured data, demonstrating the generality of the approach across different domains and data modalities.

6. Practical Takeaways

When to Use H-SCL
  • You have labels — H-SCL requires class labels to filter false negatives
  • Large batch sizes — The theoretical guarantees strengthen with more negatives; use large batches (256+)
  • Choosing hardening: Start with exponential ($\beta = 1$) for smooth hardening, or threshold ($\tau = e^{-0.5}$) for aggressive filtering
  • Multi-class problems — The biggest gains appear on fine-grained tasks (CIFAR-100 > CIFAR-10) where hard negatives matter most

7. Connection to Our Later Work

This paper laid the foundation for our subsequent TMLR paper, "Hard-Negative Sampling for Contrastive Learning: Optimal Representation Geometry and Neural- vs Dimensional-Collapse", which takes the next step: proving that H-SCL losses are minimized by Neural Collapse geometry, and showing that hard negatives help representations converge to this optimal structure. Together, these two papers provide a complete picture:

  1. This paper: H-SCL outperforms SCL; H-UCL bounds H-SCL (the "why it works")
  2. TMLR paper: The optimal geometry is Neural Collapse for all variants; hard negatives + normalization achieve it in practice (the "what it converges to")

8. Summary

  • H-SCL = labels + hard negatives — the best of both worlds
  • Avoids false negatives that plague H-UCL while focusing on informative negatives that SCL ignores
  • Theoretically justified: H-UCL loss upper bounds H-SCL loss, explaining why unsupervised hard-negative methods work
  • Practically effective: consistent gains on image and graph benchmarks, especially on fine-grained tasks
  • The hardening function $\eta$ provides a flexible knob to control the difficulty of negative samples
