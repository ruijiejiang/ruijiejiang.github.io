Delta Knowledge Distillation for Large Language Models

Ruijie Jiang · September 2025 · 10 min read
LLM Knowledge Distillation NLP LinkedIn

This post covers our paper "Delta Knowledge Distillation for Large Language Models" (Cao, Kang, Xing, Jiang, 2025). The core question: when distilling a large language model into a smaller one, should the student try to match the teacher's absolute output — or should it learn the teacher's behavioral transformation?

1. The Problem with Standard KD

Knowledge distillation (KD) for LLMs typically works by minimizing the KL divergence between the teacher's output distribution and the student's output distribution at each token:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{KD}} = \mathbb{E}\left[D_{\text{KL}}\left(\pi_t(\cdot | x) \;\|\; \pi_\theta(\cdot | x)\right)\right]$$

This implicitly assumes that the student and teacher operate in the same representation space — that the student can, in principle, match the teacher's output distribution. But when compressing a 7B-parameter model into a 1.5B model, this assumption breaks down. The student simply doesn't have enough capacity to replicate the teacher's exact behavior.

The Capacity Mismatch Problem

Forcing a small student to match a large teacher's absolute distribution is like asking a sketch artist to replicate a photograph pixel-by-pixel. Instead, the student should learn the essence of what changed during the teacher's training.

2. The Delta-KD Idea

Instead of matching the teacher's output directly, Delta-KD asks: what distributional shift did the teacher undergo during supervised fine-tuning (SFT)? Then it constructs a synthetic target that applies an analogous shift to the student's starting point.

We have four distributions available:

  • $\pi_s^{\text{raw}}(y|x)$ — student before fine-tuning (small pretrained model)
  • $\pi_t^{\text{raw}}(y|x)$ — teacher before fine-tuning (large pretrained model)
  • $\pi_t^{\text{ft}}(y|x)$ — teacher after fine-tuning (large fine-tuned model)
  • $\pi_s^*(y|x)$ — the optimal student target (what we want to approximate)

The Core Principle

Define a shift operator $\Delta(p_1, p_2) = p_1(y|x) / p_2(y|x)$ that captures the relative change between two distributions. The key insight:

$$\Delta(\pi_s^*, \pi_t^{\text{ft}})(y|x) \propto \Delta(\pi_s^{\text{raw}}, \pi_t^{\text{raw}})(y|x)^\alpha$$

In words: the shift from the optimal student to the fine-tuned teacher should be proportional to the shift from the raw student to the raw teacher. This is a parallelogram relationship in log-probability space — the teacher's SFT transformation is transferred to the student, adjusted for capacity differences via the scaling factor $\alpha$.

3. The Delta-KD Loss

Solving for the optimal student target $\pi_s^*$ gives:

$$\pi_s^*(y_t | x) = \frac{1}{Z(x, y_{<t})} \cdot \pi_t^{\text{ft}}(y_t | x) \cdot \left(\frac{\pi_s^{\text{raw}}(y_t | x)}{\pi_t^{\text{raw}}(y_t | x)}\right)^\alpha$$

where $Z(x, y_{<t})$ is a token-level normalization constant. The Delta-KD loss then distills into this synthetic target:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{Delta-KD}} = \mathbb{E}\left[D_{\text{KL}}\left(\pi_s^*(\cdot | x) \;\|\; \pi_\theta(\cdot | x)\right)\right]$$

The final training objective combines this with standard SFT:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{SFT}} + (1 - \lambda) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{Delta-KD}}$$
Intuition: Why Delta Works

Standard KD says: "student, become the teacher." Delta-KD says: "student, change the same way the teacher changed." The latter is achievable even when the student is much smaller, because it only needs to replicate a relative transformation, not an absolute distribution.

4. Scalable Training Architecture

Delta-KD requires running three models simultaneously during training: $\pi_s^{\text{raw}}$, $\pi_t^{\text{raw}}$, and $\pi_t^{\text{ft}}$ (plus the student being trained). This creates GPU memory challenges. The paper introduces a decoupled inference-training architecture:

  • Teacher models run as standalone inference services on dedicated GPUs
  • Student training communicates with teachers via ZeroMQ sockets
  • Logits are transferred through shared memory in FP16 format
  • Eliminates costly offline precomputation while avoiding memory overflow

This is trained on 8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs — a practical setup for production environments at LinkedIn.

6. The Parallelogram Framework

The paper explores a generalized Parallelogram Loss framework with 8 variants (V1-V8), combining different choices of student distributions, starting points, and shift terms. Key findings:

  • V1 (standard Delta-KD) and V6 perform best (~0.54 ROUGE-1 on UltraChat)
  • Half the variants (V3, V5, V7, V8) diverge during training due to excessive gradient norm growth — when gradients flow through the shift terms, they can explode
  • The stable variants are those where the shift term is treated as a fixed constant (no gradient), not a differentiable quantity
Practical Insight

Not all parallelogram configurations work. The key to stability is treating the distribution shift ratio $(\pi_s^{\text{raw}} / \pi_t^{\text{raw}})^\alpha$ as a detached constant during backpropagation. Letting gradients flow through this term causes divergence.

7. Why This Matters for Production LLMs

At LinkedIn, we deploy LLMs at massive scale for recommendations, ads, and search. Serving a 7B model per request is prohibitively expensive. Knowledge distillation into smaller models (1.5B, 500M) is essential. Delta-KD provides:

  • Better quality at the same model size — more teacher knowledge preserved
  • Principled framework — not just a heuristic, but grounded in the parallelogram relationship
  • Scalable training — the decoupled architecture handles multi-model training on standard GPU clusters
  • Domain generality — works on both instruction-following and math reasoning, suggesting broad applicability

8. Key Takeaways

Summary
  • Don't match the teacher's output — instead, match the teacher's behavioral transformation during SFT
  • The delta formulation $\pi_s^* \propto \pi_t^{\text{ft}} \cdot (\pi_s^{\text{raw}} / \pi_t^{\text{raw}})^\alpha$ elegantly accounts for capacity mismatch
  • Consistent wins over Forward KL, Reverse KL, SeqKD, and MiniLLM on both instruction-following and reasoning tasks
  • Scaling factor $\alpha$ and loss weight $\lambda$ provide tunable knobs for different teacher-student size ratios
  • Stability matters: detach the shift ratio from the computation graph to avoid gradient explosion
