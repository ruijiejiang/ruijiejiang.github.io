Hard-Negative Sampling for Contrastive Learning: Understanding Neural Collapse vs Dimensional Collapse

Ruijie Jiang · March 15, 2025 · 12 min read
Contrastive Learning Neural Collapse Representation Learning Theory

This post provides a deep dive into our TMLR paper "Hard-Negative Sampling for Contrastive Learning: Optimal Representation Geometry and Neural- vs Dimensional-Collapse" (Jiang, Nguyen, Aeron, Ishwar, 2025). We ask a fundamental question: what is the optimal geometric structure of learned representations in contrastive learning, and how does hard-negative sampling affect it?

1. The Big Picture

Contrastive learning has become the dominant paradigm for self-supervised representation learning. The core idea is simple: pull positive pairs (augmented views of the same data) together while pushing negative pairs apart. But a critical design choice is how to select negative samples.

Hard-negative sampling — preferentially selecting negatives that are similar to the anchor — is widely used in practice because it speeds up convergence and improves downstream performance. Yet we lack a rigorous theoretical understanding of why it works and what geometric structure it induces.

This paper bridges that gap by proving that both standard and hard-negative contrastive losses share the same globally optimal geometry: Neural Collapse (NC). But the path to reaching that optimum is very different — and that's where things get interesting.

2. What is Neural Collapse?

Neural Collapse, first observed in the supervised classification setting (Papyan et al., 2020), describes a striking phenomenon where learned representations converge to a highly symmetric structure as training progresses:

  • Within-class variability vanishes — all samples from the same class map to the same point
  • Class means form an Equiangular Tight Frame (ETF) — they are maximally and equally separated on the unit hypersphere
  • The inner product between any two distinct class means is exactly $-\frac{1}{C-1}$, where $C$ is the number of classes

Think of it as placing $C$ points on a sphere so they're as far apart from each other as possible, with every pair having equal angular separation. For $C=3$ this gives a regular triangle; for $C=4$, a regular tetrahedron.

Key Intuition

Neural Collapse is the "most spread out" configuration possible. It maximizes inter-class separation while maintaining perfect symmetry — exactly what we'd want for good representations.

3. The Theoretical Framework

3.1 Generalized Contrastive Loss

Rather than analyzing only InfoNCE, the paper works with a general family of contrastive losses:

$$\ell_k(z, z^+, z_1^-, \ldots, z_k^-) = \psi_k\big(z^\top(z_1^- - z^+), \ldots, z^\top(z_k^- - z^+)\big)$$

where $\psi_k$ is any convex, argument-wise non-decreasing function. This encompasses InfoNCE, triplet loss, and many other common objectives. The key property: the loss penalizes situations where negatives are closer to the anchor than positives.

3.2 Hard-Negative Sampling

Standard contrastive learning draws negatives uniformly (or from the class-conditional distribution). Hard-negative sampling reweights the distribution to favor negatives that are more similar to the anchor:

$$p_{\text{HSCL}}^-(x_1^-, \ldots, x_k^- \mid x, y, f) = \frac{\eta\big(f(x)^\top f(x_1^-), \ldots, f(x)^\top f(x_k^-)\big)}{\gamma(x,y,f)} \cdot \prod_{i=1}^{k} p_{\text{SCL}}^-(x_i^- \mid y)$$

The hardening function $\eta$ upweights negatives whose representations are more aligned with the anchor. This makes the contrastive task harder, forcing the encoder to learn more discriminative features.

4. Main Results

Theorem 1 — Hard losses lower-bound standard losses

For all representation functions $f$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{HSCL}}^{(k)}(f) \geq \mathcal{L}_{\text{SCL}}^{(k)}(f) \quad \text{and} \quad \mathcal{L}_{\text{HUCL}}^{(k)}(f) \geq \mathcal{L}_{\text{UCL}}^{(k)}(f)$$

Hard-negative contrastive losses are always at least as large as their standard counterparts for any encoder. This is proved via the Harris inequality (a multivariate association inequality).

This result is intuitive: hard negatives make the task harder, so the loss must be higher. But the proof is non-trivial — it requires showing a positive association property between the hardening function and the loss, which the authors establish via the Harris comparison inequality.

Theorem 2 — SCL is minimized at Neural Collapse

The SCL loss achieves its global minimum if and only if the representations satisfy Neural Collapse:

$$\mu_j^\top \mu_\ell = -\frac{1}{C-1} \quad \forall\ j \neq \ell$$

where $\mu_j$ are the class mean representations. This holds for any loss function in the general family — not just InfoNCE.

Theorem 3 — UCL also converges to NC

Unsupervised contrastive losses are also minimized by NC geometry, provided the embedding dimension $d \geq C - 1$ (where $C$ is the number of latent classes). This extends NC theory beyond the supervised setting.

Why This Matters

Both standard and hard variants of contrastive learning share the same optimal geometry (NC). But Theorem 1 tells us hard-negative losses create steeper gradients away from the optimum — which intuitively explains why hard-negative sampling speeds up convergence in practice.

5. Neural Collapse vs Dimensional Collapse

The theory predicts NC as the optimum — but can SGD actually reach it? This is where the paper's empirical contribution shines. The experiments reveal a critical dichotomy:

Dimensional Collapse (Bad)

Without hard-negative sampling or feature normalization, learned representations suffer from Dimensional Collapse (DC) — the representation matrix becomes low-rank, meaning the encoder only uses a small fraction of the available dimensions. Information is lost, and downstream performance degrades.

Neural Collapse (Good)

With hard-negative sampling AND feature normalization (projecting representations to the unit sphere), the representations converge to Neural Collapse — the theoretically optimal geometry. Both ingredients are necessary:

  • Hard negatives alone — not sufficient; DC can still occur
  • Normalization alone — not sufficient; representations may not converge to ETF
  • Hard negatives + normalization — consistently achieves NC geometry

This is validated across synthetic data and real datasets (CIFAR-10, etc.) with varying batch sizes (256, 512, 2048), different hardening functions, and multiple optimization settings.

6. Technical Highlights

Cleaner Proofs

A notable methodological contribution: previous work proving NC optimality for contrastive learning required conditional independence between augmented views given the class. This paper removes that assumption, providing simpler, more transparent proofs that apply to realistic augmentation pipelines where the positive pair $(x, x^+)$ can be arbitrarily dependent.

Practical Batched Losses

The theoretical guarantees extend to batched empirical losses — the actual objectives minimized in practice. This bridges the gap between the idealized infinite-sample theory and real mini-batch training.

7. Key Takeaways

Summary
  • Neural Collapse is the universal optimum for both supervised and unsupervised contrastive learning, with or without hard negatives, for a general family of loss functions.
  • Hard-negative sampling creates higher losses (steeper optimization landscape), which explains its empirical benefit — stronger gradients push representations toward NC faster.
  • Feature normalization is critical — without it, training suffers from Dimensional Collapse regardless of hard-negative sampling.
  • The combination of hard negatives + normalization is key to achieving the theoretical optimum in practice.
  • These results hold for any convex, monotone contrastive loss — not just InfoNCE — making them broadly applicable.

8. Open Questions

Several interesting directions remain:

  • Can we characterize the convergence rate to NC as a function of negative hardness?
  • How does the optimal hardening schedule change during training?
  • What is the relationship between NC geometry and downstream transfer learning performance?
  • Can these results extend to non-contrastive self-supervised methods (BYOL, VICReg)?
